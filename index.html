Film Festival Exhibition

Telling Chinese Stories to the World

Cultural Soft Power and Confidence on Screen

This exhibition explores how Chinese cinema speaks to global audiences through different genres, emotional registers, and cultural values. Across martial arts, science fiction, war, historical drama, and fantasy romance, five films reveal a plural, confident, and deeply human image of China on screen.

Theme

A confident culture never speaks in only one voice. These films together present China as diverse, emotionally rich, and culturally self-assured.

Curatorial Lens

The exhibition understands soft power as attraction rather than coercion: the power to move, resonate, and build understanding through stories.

Curatorial Statement

This English version adapts the original Chinese curatorial text into a concise, internationally accessible exhibition statement.

A self-assured culture never speaks in only one voice.

As Chinese cinema enters the global stage, it does not carry a single image or a fixed narrative. Instead, it brings a rich spectrum of stories, emotions, and visual languages. This festival, themed Telling Chinese Stories to the World: Cultural Soft Power and Confidence on Screen, presents five films across kung fu action, science fiction disaster, modern war, historical drama, and fantasy romance. Together, they portray a diverse, layered, and confident China.

Cultural confidence appears here in different forms. It is the quiet discipline of Wing Chun, speaking to the world through an ethic of restraint and dignity. It is the decision to carry Earth itself into the future, expressing a collective attachment to home and shared destiny. It is the image of a soldier defending borders and people, transforming strength into responsibility. It is the warmth of ordinary individuals whose lives humanize national history. And it is a poetic love story that crosses the boundary between human and spirit, asking universal questions about love, sacrifice, desire, and truth.

These five films show that Chinese stories do not need to wait passively to be viewed by others. They actively enter global cinema through recognizable genres while speaking in culturally distinct voices: kung fu engages tradition, science fiction imagines the future, war narratives frame duty and protection, historical drama reveals emotional warmth, and fantasy romance builds cross-cultural intimacy. This reflects the true essence of soft power: not influence by force, but resonance through attraction.

To tell Chinese stories to the world is also to express cultural confidence with calm clarity. This festival invites audiences to experience five cinematic voices and witness a China that speaks for itself with imagination, dignity, and emotional depth.

Selected Films

Five films, five genres, five cinematic echoes of Chinese cultural soft power.

Kung Fu Action

Ip Man (2008)

Director: Wilson Yip | Genre: Martial Arts / Biography

Set in 1930s Foshan during national crisis, Ip Man transforms martial arts into a language of dignity, restraint, and moral strength. It presents kung fu not as exotic spectacle, but as a bearer of Chinese philosophical values.

The film demonstrates how traditional culture becomes soft power when it is expressed through virtue, calmness, and ethical force rather than violence alone.

Sci-Fi Disaster

The Wandering Earth (2019)

Director: Frant Gwo | Genre: Science Fiction / Disaster

Instead of abandoning Earth, humanity in this film chooses to move the planet itself. That imaginative premise reflects a deeply rooted cultural attachment to home, continuity, and collective destiny.

Through the universal language of science fiction, the film communicates a distinctly Chinese value system: in a crisis, home is not discarded, but carried forward together.

Modern War

Wolf Warrior (2015)

Director: Wu Jing | Genre: Action / War

Centered on a former special forces soldier defending the border, Wolf Warrior frames national strength through protection, duty, and responsibility rather than conquest.

Its global action language carries a specifically Chinese narrative of guardianship: power is meaningful when it safeguards people and territory, not when it dominates others.

Historical Drama

My People, My Country (2019)

Directors: Multiple | Genre: Drama / Anthology

Through seven historical moments and the stories of ordinary people, this film turns national memory into intimate human experience.

Its emotional strength lies in warmth rather than grandeur. By showing how history is lived through everyday lives, the film offers a gentle yet powerful form of cultural confidence.

Fantasy Romance

Painted Skin (2008)

Director: Gordon Chan | Genre: Fantasy / Romance

Adapted from classical Chinese literature, Painted Skin uses a supernatural love triangle to explore sincerity, desire, sacrifice, and moral conflict.

The film shows that Chinese soft power does not depend only on large historical narratives. A poetic, emotionally complex story can also create profound cross-cultural connection.

Narrative Arc

From a martial arts hall in Foshan to a frozen planet in motion, from the borderlands of national defense to the alleys of urban memory, and finally to a supernatural romance in the desert, these films create a full cinematic spectrum.

Together they offer an answer to a contemporary question: what does cultural soft power look like on screen? It appears as martial ethics, attachment to home, national responsibility, human warmth, and emotional generosity.

This is the confident echo of Chinese storytelling on screen: not one fixed image, but many voices speaking with clarity, dignity, and resonance.
